    Section 1.1 Overall

    Number theory is the study of properties of integers (and rational numbers). Often, we study prime numbers
    ðŸ”—
    \begin{equation*} 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, \ldots \end{equation*}
    which are positive integers greater than \(1\) that can be divided only by \(1\) and itself.
    ðŸ”—
    1. Why are primes important?
      ðŸ”—
      They are the building blocks of integers. To be precise, every natural number can be written as a product of primes. This is called the Fundamental Theorem of Arithmetic.
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    2. How can we find primes?
      ðŸ”—
      Use sieve of Eratosthenes (pronounced "eh-ruh-taas-thuh-neez").

      ðŸ”—
    3. How many primes are there?
      ðŸ”—
      Euclid proved that there are infinitely many primes. To be more precise, he proved that given a finite number of primes \(p_1, \ldots, p_n\text{,}\) at least one of the prime divisors of \(p_1 \cdots p_n + 1\) will be a new prime \(p_{n+1}\) that is not \(p_1, \ldots, p_n\text{.}\)
      ðŸ”—
      ðŸ”—
    4. How many primes are there \(\leq x\text{?}\)
      ðŸ”—
      Denote by \(\pi(x)\) the number of primes less or equal to \(x\text{.}\) In general, computing \(\pi(x)\) is difficult, but roughly
      ðŸ”—
      \begin{equation*} \pi(x) \sim \dfrac{x}{\log x} \end{equation*}
      This is called the Prime Number Theorem. Informally, this means that the chance of a random positive integer \(n\) being a prime is approximately \(\dfrac{1}{\log n}\text{.}\)

      ðŸ”—

    7. Congruences
      ðŸ”—
      • Is there a solution to \(x^2 = 12351523134221517\text{?}\) Only possible last digit of \(x^2\) is \(0, 1, 4, 5, 6, 9\text{.}\)
        ðŸ”—
        ðŸ”—
      • We say that \(a \equiv b \pmod{m}\) if \(a-b\) is divisible by \(m\text{.}\) Roughly, we ignore multiples of \(m\text{.}\) Here we used the fact that \(x^2 \equiv 0, 1, 4, 5, 6, 9 \pmod{10}\text{.}\)

        ðŸ”—

For example, we check \(2^m \equiv 2 \pmod{m}\text{.}\) If \(2^m \not\equiv 2 \pmod{m}\text{,}\) then \(m\) is NOT a prime. Cryptography depends on large primes, so testing primality is important.

    8. Quadratic Residues
      ðŸ”—
      \(a\) is a quadratic residue modulo \(m\) if \(x^2 \equiv a \pmod{m}\) is solvable. In simpler terms, \(a\) is a square modulo \(m\text{.}\) If \(m=5\text{,}\) then \(1, 4, 6, 9, 11, 14, \ldots\) are quadratic residues modulo \(m\text{,}\) and \(2, 3, 5, 8, 10, 12, 13, \ldots\) are not quadratic residues modulo \(m\text{.}\) There is a strong connection between \(x^2 \equiv p \pmod{q}\) and \(x^2 \equiv q \pmod{p}\) called Quadratic Reciprocity. If \(p\) or \(q\) is \(1 \pmod{4}\text{,}\) then either BOTH or NEITHER equations can be solved. If \(p \equiv q \equiv 3 \pmod{4}\text{,}\) then EXACTLY one solution can be solved. For example, \(101\) is a square modulo \(5\text{.}\) Then Quadratic Reciprocity says that \(5\) is square modulo \(101\text{.}\)

      ðŸ”—

        ðŸ”—
